Context
02

Why This Matters

DTC brands generate more data than most enterprises. The problem is not access. It is structure. Shopify gives you orders. Ad platforms give you spend. Email gives you sends. None of it is naturally consistent.

The symptoms are familiar. CAC to LTV ratios that look fine but do not match cash flow. Retention programs that are never measured with a holdout. Forecasts that miss every quarter. Attribution that credits whatever touched last.

The root cause is definition drift. Every tool defines revenue, conversions, and customers slightly differently. A dashboard can be precise and still be wrong.

Maturity is not tools. It is the set of questions you can answer with a straight face, and the audit trail behind the answer.

  • Reporting: What happened.
  • Diagnosis: What changed, and where.
  • Prediction: What is likely next, with uncertainty.
  • Optimization: What to do next, and what it costs to be wrong.

Data Endpoints and Tables

Most teams talk about analytics maturity without naming the inputs. We prefer the opposite. If you cannot point to the raw tables, you do not own the metric.

Rule of thumb: start from order level truth, then build outward. Spend and sessions are secondary. Everything is definition work.

  • Order truth: orders, line items, discounts, refunds, tax, shipping, payment status.
  • Customer truth: customer id stitching, first order date, acquisition assignment, lifecycle events.
  • Spend truth: daily spend by campaign, with stable mapping to channels.
  • Context truth: promos, price changes, stockouts, site incidents, shipping delays.

Methods
06

Soft Methodologies That Keep You Honest

Most analytics stacks fail quietly. They look fine until the first hard decision. These are the practices that prevent that.

None of this is exotic. It is disciplined definitions, reconciliation, and validation. Boring on purpose.

Metric Contracts
You define revenue, orders, customers, and new vs returning once, then enforce it everywhere. This is how you stop dashboard debates from repeating every month.
  • Revenue definition: gross or net, include tax, include shipping, refund timing.
  • Customer definition: identity stitching rules and fallbacks.
  • Attribution policy: windows documented and stable.
Reconciliation
Every key series must reconcile to a source of truth within a tolerance. If it does not, you do not model it. You fix it.
  • Orders reconcile to Shopify payouts or Stripe settlements.
  • Spend reconciles to invoices, not dashboards.
  • Timezones and currency conversions are explicit.
Cohorts Before Models
Most LTV mistakes are cohort mistakes. If cohorts are wrong, prediction multiplies the error.
  • Cohort key: first order date or first paid touch, chosen explicitly.
  • Tracking drift: acquisition source changes over time get logged.
  • Outputs: LTV at fixed horizons, retention curves, payback curves.
Predictive LTV That Admits Uncertainty
Predictive LTV is useful when it is calibrated. The goal is not perfect prediction. The goal is better thresholds and better tradeoffs.
  • Separate observed value from predicted future value.
  • Use calibration checks on holdout cohorts.
  • Report ranges, not point estimates, when uncertainty is high.
Forecasting With Intervals
A single number forecast is a story. A forecast with intervals is a decision tool.
  • Seasonality and promo effects are explicit inputs.
  • Scenarios model constraints like inventory and cash.
  • Backtests are done on prior periods, not curated weeks.
Allocation With Validation
Attribution is fast. MMM is wide. Tests are causal. Mature teams use all three and resolve disagreement with a test.
  • Model diminishing returns with response curves.
  • Run holdouts for claims that move budget.
  • Use the test result to calibrate planning assumptions.

Plain rule: if a decision is expensive, you buy certainty with a test. If a decision is frequent, you buy speed with directional indicators. If a decision is strategic, you buy coverage with an allocation model.

Sequence
08

What To Build, In Order

Sequence matters. Predictive work built on broken cohorts is worse than no model. Allocation work built on unreconciled spend is fantasy.

Phase 1

Foundation

  • Warehouse with raw tables and history
  • ETL from Shopify, ad platforms, email, analytics
  • Channel mapping table you control
  • Promo calendar and operational context table
  • Reconciliation checks as a scheduled job
4 to 6 weeks
Phase 2

Unit Economics

  • Cohort table with acquisition assignment rules
  • LTV at fixed horizons by cohort and channel
  • Payback curves by acquisition source
  • Retention curves by segment
  • Margin aware targets, not revenue only targets
3 to 4 weeks
Phase 3

Prediction

  • Predictive LTV with calibration checks
  • Churn risk scores tied to interventions
  • Forecast with intervals and scenario inputs
  • Refresh and monitoring schedule
  • Model documentation that survives handoff
4 to 6 weeks
Phase 4

Optimization

  • MMM for allocation with response curves
  • Incrementality testing playbook and capability
  • Scenario planning that ties spend to distributions
  • Calibration loop that updates assumptions
  • Decision memos tied to tradeoffs and constraints
6 to 8 weeks

The trap is skipping Phase 2.

Most brands want to jump from dashboards to prediction. Predictive models trained on bad cohorts do not fail loudly. They fail by producing confident targets that move budget in the wrong direction. Fix cohort economics first. Everything downstream depends on it.

Perspective
09

Our Take

Most brands overestimate their level. They have dashboards, so they assume they are mature. If decisions still happen in spreadsheets or gut feel, you are still early. You just have more charts.

Start where your decision pain is. If you are spending hard on acquisition, Phase 2 is usually the first compounding move. Cohorts, payback, and margin aware targets.

Do not confuse attribution with truth. Attribution is useful for speed inside digital. It does not prove incrementality. When the decision is expensive, validate it.

Analytics maturity is ownership. If you can rebuild the metric, you can trust it. If you cannot, you are renting it.

Common Failure
Multiple revenue numbers. Nobody knows which one is net of refunds. Strategy drifts because the baseline is not stable.
Quick Win
Build cohorts and ltv_by_horizon. Plot retention and payback by acquisition month. Most teams learn more from this than from another dashboard.
When Level 4 Pays
When you have enough history and enough spend that allocation errors are expensive, and you need response curves and validation, not stories.
